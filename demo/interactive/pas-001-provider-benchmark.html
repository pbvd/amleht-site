01 · Three Questions. Three Answers.

Clarisse did not ask a commercial question. She asked a structural one — about process, governance, and institutional responsibility. Each question has a direct answer from an independent assessor operating under the Thelma Principle: assessed exclusively on behalf of institutions.

Question One
"Why was there no benchmark including European providers?"
The benchmark exists. DORA Article 28 mandates that regulated institutions maintain a register of ICT third-party service providers and conduct documented risk assessments before any critical dependency is established. The framework for evaluating any provider — European or otherwise — against jurisdiction, operational resilience, concentration risk, and contractual control is already in law. What was missing was an independent party applying it without commercial interest in the outcome.
Question Two
"Why promote a single vendor across multiple use cases?"
Concentration risk is a DORA Art.28(8) concern, not an opinion. Where an institution — or a consortium of institutions — becomes critically dependent on a single ICT third-party provider, the competent authority must be notified and ongoing monitoring demonstrated. Multi-vendor diversity is not a preference; it is a regulatory expectation. An independent assessor flags single-vendor concentration before contracts are signed, not after.
Question Three
"This is not about one vendor versus another. It is about the direction we choose collectively."
Correct — and that is precisely why assessment cannot come from a vendor. The direction is set by institutions exercising informed, independent judgment. That judgment requires a compliance layer that sits above infrastructure providers, assesses them impartially, and reports exclusively to the institution. Not to the provider selected. Not to the consortium lead. To the institution.
02 · The Benchmark Already Exists — The EC Published It

The European Commission answered Clarisse's question before she asked it. In April 2026, the Commission awarded €180 million of cloud infrastructure contracts using a published, scored, procurement-grade sovereignty standard called SEAL — Sovereignty Effectiveness Assurance Levels. It is not internal guidance. It is not a think-tank framework. It is the standard the Commission itself applied to its own critical infrastructure procurement.

SEAL runs from 0 to 4. SEAL-2 was the minimum threshold for eligibility. Three of four awarded providers reached SEAL-3. The framework covers eight dimensions. Supply chain sovereignty carries the highest scoring weight at 20%.

amleht applies this framework — alongside DORA Art.28 — to any ICT infrastructure provider on behalf of a regulated institution. It is not our benchmark. It is the EC's. We are the independent party applying it.

SEAL-0 · NO SOVEREIGNTY INELIGIBLE FOR EU CRITICAL INFRASTRUCTURE
Operations under full non-EU control. CLOUD Act or equivalent foreign jurisdiction applies. No enforceable EU law protection over data or operations. US-incorporated providers with US-domiciled infrastructure default to this tier.
SEAL-2 · DATA SOVEREIGNTY MINIMUM EC ELIGIBILITY THRESHOLD
EU law is enforceable over data at rest and in transit. However material non-EU dependencies remain — in supply chain, tooling, or operational control. Meets minimum bar but does not eliminate foreign influence on critical operations.
SEAL-3 · DIGITAL RESILIENCE ACHIEVED BY 3 OF 4 EC-AWARDED PROVIDERS
EU actors exercise meaningful operational control. Non-EU influence is marginal and documented. Supply chain provenance verified. Technology openness demonstrated. Security operations under EU governance. This is the target tier for systemic European financial infrastructure.
SEAL-4 · FULL DIGITAL SOVEREIGNTY COMPLETE EU CONTROL · NO NON-EU DEPENDENCIES
Complete EU control across all eight dimensions. No critical non-EU dependencies anywhere in the stack. The highest achievable tier. Currently rare — represents the long-term target for European sovereign infrastructure.

04 · The Independence Principle

Clarisse's open letter identified a structural problem: the absence of an independent assessor. That absence is not an accident — it is the default state when compliance services are offered by parties with commercial relationships to the providers they assess.

amleht was built with one structural constraint that resolves this:

The Thelma Principle — Foundational · Non-Negotiable
amleht assesses ICT third-party providers exclusively on behalf of EU financial institution clients. amleht does not accept advisory, remediation, consultancy, or certification engagements from providers it assesses or may assess.

This is not a policy. It is a structural characteristic of the business. It does not bend. It does not have exceptions.
